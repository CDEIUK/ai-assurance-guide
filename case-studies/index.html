CAHAIImpact assessmentPrivate and Public sector actors in the AI supply chain.

CAHAI (the Council of Europe’s Committee on AI and Human Rights) are producing an impact assessment for organisations developing, procuring or deploying AI to identify and mitigate the potential impacts of AI systems on human rights, democracy and the rule of law.

The impact assessment combines elements of Algorithm impact assessment with the human rights due diligence process. The impact assessment combines an analysis of potential risks of AI systems with an assessment of the potential impacts of these risks on human rights, democracy and the rule of law. The impact assessment involves stakeholder engagement and assessment of the governance and mitigation measures.

The impact assessment will support a potentially legally binding instrument to be produced by the Council of Europe, governing the use of AI.

Currently at the drafting stage by the CAHAI Policy Development Group (PDG)
ORCAAAI risk managementCompanies procuring / deploying AI systems

ORCAA’s algorithm audits identify and manage risks arising from the use of predictive models, focusing on ethical dimensions including fairness, bias and discrimination.

ORCAA’s algorithm audit comprises 4 key steps:

  1. Preparation - defining the use case and reviewing background documentation
  2. Stakeholder discussions - to elicit the concerns of internal and external stakeholders.
  3. The ethical matrix - mapping stakeholders and their concerns onto a grid to prioritise the most pressing concerns.
  4. Planning remediation steps - coming up with ways to investigate, validate and address the priority concerns identified. Reviewing companies, preparation, validation and testing practices.

ParityCompliance and risk assessmentOrganisations developing or deploying AI systems.

Parity provides a 3rd party model risk assessment platform for considering compliance, risk and impact of an AI system.

Parity offers a range of tools beginning with an impact assessment to bias mitigation tools, continuous algorithm monitoring and explainability platforms.

The parity enables organisations to connect up their model cards to their platform to provide model documentation for analysis. The platform first asks the organisation to build an impact assessment which is used to identify risk zones, algorithm assessment methods and methods to mitigate harmful model qualities like disparate impact.

ForHumanityIndependent Audit for AI systems (IAAIS)Audit rules are applicable to both public and private entities but the comprehensive process is targeted at publicly traded companies.

ForHumanity’s approach to Independent Audit for AI Systems provides a framework to audit AI Safety in the categories of Bias, Privacy, Ethics, Trust and Cybersecurity.

The aim of the audit is to build an ‘infrastructure of trust’. To create this infrastructure of trust, ForHumanity have set out five criteria to ensure that rules are auditable:

  1. Binary - compliant/non-compliant
  2. Measurable/unambiguous
  3. Iterated and open source
  4. Consensus driven
  5. Implementable

ForHumanity emphasise independence and auditability as two key components in building trust:

  • Auditability ensures that organisations can demonstrate and communicate compliance.
  • Independence ensures that an audit practitioner has to attest to compliance, ensuring accountability (because they have something to lose if they are wrong)

In use
ForHumanityUKGDPR Certification frameworkData processors, controllers and joint controllers

ForHumanity’s UKGDPR Certification framework responded to the ICO’s call to develop a certification scheme for UKGDPR compliance. It is the first of its kind.

The certification framework can be applied to all AI and data driven systems where personal data is being processed.

A critical aspect of ForHumanity’s certification scheme is robust governance, oversight and accountability at the Officer and Board of Directors level to ensure trust. Simply requiring an organisation to comply with certification criteria without defining specific accountability in governance structures creates ambiguity regarding delegation of authority and/or segregation of duties.

The certification scheme requires the establishment of an Algorithmic Risk Committee, Ethics Committee and Children’s Data Oversight committees to ensure conflict free, objective decision-making.

ForHumanity audit criteria is designed to ensure human agency is embedded in all algorithmic systems, Artificial Intelligence and Autonomous systems.

Submitted to the ICO
SHERPA ProjectBaseline model for AI impact assessment EU-based developers or users of AI systems

The Sherpa project’s aim is to develop baseline recommendations for the Eurpoean commission for developing an AI impact assessment.

The SHERPA project has put forward recommendations for guidance in four key areas:

  1. Processes for conducting and evaluating AI impact assessments
  2. Measures and metrics for AI impact assessment
  3. Determination of risk level for a technology or application
  4. Issues to be included in an AI impact assessment

They also recommend that AI impact assessment should build on numerous existing impact assessments e.g. data protection, human rights, ethics, environmental and socio-economic impact assessment.

To accompany the recommendations, SHERPA has submitted accounts of the ethical and human rights issues of AI as part of its case study research. They have also built likely future scenarios to inform design.

Government of CanadaAlgorithm impact assessment toolGovernment departments and agencies

The Algorithm Impact Assessment (AIA) is a mandatory questionnaire that determines the impact level of an automated decision-system.

The tool is composed of 48 risk and 33 mitigation questions. Assessment scores are based on factors including the system design, algorithm, decision type, impact and data.

The assessment is organised according to the Canadian Government’s policy, ethical, and administrative law considerations of automated decision making risk areas. It is designed to help departments and agencies better understand and manage AI risks.

The tool outlines 6 key risk areas:

  1. Project
  2. System
  3. Algorithm
  4. Decision
  5. Impact
  6. Data

The tool outlines two further mitigation areas:

  1. Consultation
  2. De-risking and mitigation measures

The value of each question in the impact assessment is weighted based on the raw impact and mitigation.

Completion of the AIA indicates an impact level for the system in question. Impact levels range from little impact (level 1) to very high impact (level 4)

Responsible Artificial Intelligence InstituteRAI CertificationPractitioners creating responsible AI

RAI certification is used by organisations to demonstrate that an AI system has been designed, built, and deployed in line with the five OECD Principles on Artificial Intelligence:

  1. Explainability
  2. Fairness
  3. Accountability
  4. Robustness
  5. Data Quality

The five OECD categories of responsible AI are used as parameters for the different credit elements within the RAI Certification rating system.

FiddlerMachine learning monitoring and explainable AIBusinesses developing / deploying AI

Fiddlers platform offers continuous ML monitoring and automated AI explainability for an organisation to monitor, observe, and analyse their AI models.

Fiddlers platform send real time alerts to users when models behave unexpectedly, providing actionable insights. The platform enables users to pinpoint data drift, including contributing features to alert users when they need to retarian their models and highlights model inference violations when they happen for proactive mitigation.

Fiddler’s methodology is based on a feedback loop beginning with offline steps:

  1. Train - Logging training and test datasets to check bias and feature quality
  2. Validate - Explain the model, discover low performance and create models dashboards and reports

This is followed by three online steps:

  1. Deploy - Recoring model traffic and metadata, comparing new and existing models.
  2. Monitor - Observing performance drift, outliers, bias and errors on conditions and slices.
  3. Analyse - Analyse performance issues, identify root causes, generate adversarial examples.

EthicsGradeEnvironmental, Social and Governance (ESG) ratingsBusinesses using AI

EthicsGrade, grade and score companies on their AI governance. They produce a publicly available scorecard based on their assessments which are based on ESG responsibilities.

EthicsGrade use a three step process to generate a scorecard:

  1. Collect - Information is collected from publicly available resources, information submitted by the company through a survey, and through internal assessment.
  2. Analyse - Information is analysed through 6 research dimensions. Structure, public policy, technical barriers to trust, ethical risk, data privacy, sustainability.
  3. Grade - Three types of scorecard are updated based on available information: Baseline scorecard, InsideView Scorecard and Benchmark Scorecard.

AI Now InstituteAlgorithm Impact Assessment (based on environmental impact assessment)Public Agencies

AI Now Institute’s Impact Assessment report provides a practical framework, similar to an environmental impact assessment, for agencies to bring oversight to automated decision systems.

Their framework helps affected communities and stakeholders assess the use of AI and algorithmic decision-making in public agencies and determine where – or if – their use is acceptable.

Key elements of the Public Agency Algorithmic Impact Assessment:

  1. Agencies should conduct a self assessment of automated decision systems evaluating potential impacts on fairness, justice, bias, or other concerns across affected communities
  2. Agencies should develop meaningful external researcher review processes to measure and track impact over time.
  3. Agencies should provide notice to the public disclosing their definition of ‘automated decision system’, existing and proposed systems, and any related self-assessments before the system has been acquired.
  4. Agencies should solicit public comments to clarify concerns and answer questions.

The goal of the AI suitability toolkit is to increase NGO’s internal expertise and capacity to evaluate, develop, procure and use AI and ML in their work so that they can make informed decisions, improve their work and anticipate issues that might arise.

The toolkit includes an ‘AI Workshop for Nonprofits’ and an AI suitability framework. The workshop and materials provide participants with an overview of AI/ML and how to evaluate their suitability for projects.

DeloitteTrustworthy AI frameworkIndustry facing

Deloitte’s trustworthy AI solutions include a six pillar Trustworthy AI framework which aims to guide organizations on how to apply AI responsibly and ethically within their businesses. The six pillars are:

  1. Fair and impartial
  2. Robust and reliable
  3. Privacy
  4. Safe ans secure
  5. Responsible and accountable
  6. Transparent and explainable

Deloitte use a three stage AI risk management framework focused on:

  1. Identifying and assessing risks
  2. Ensuring existing activities meet required standards
  3. Monitoring and tracking key risk indicators

Deloitte have also developed AlgoInsight: an AI explainability toolkit to facilitate assessments of bias and performance.
